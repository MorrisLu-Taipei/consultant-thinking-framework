## Why this book is "active"
A traditional book you **read**; this book you **use**. It is bound to an AI coach (the `consulting-thinking-coach` skill): after each chapter you don't highlight passages — you **hand your next real meeting to the AI and get critiqued, scored, and shown the missing diagonal on the spot**. The book itself can be questioned and applied to your own context. That is what an AI-native ebook is: **queryable, executable, and it follows your own cases.** It is not a paper book moved onto a screen — it **binds method, practice, critique, Socratic challenge, and pre/post testing into one closed loop.**

> This book is the **reader-facing weave** of the canonical methodology modules (`10_Thinking_Framework/`). Formal citation should reference the modules + the future deposited DOI; the book's job is to make the method **readable and usable**.

## Who this book is for
This book is for three kinds of people: **consultants who are just starting to write meeting notes**, **managers who lead a consulting team**, and **sales / pre-sales who want to turn client meetings into proposal insight**. It is **not** for someone who only wants to learn a single strategy framework — what it trains is **the ability to turn frameworks into judgment**.

## How to use this book (3-minute setup)
1. Make sure you have the `consulting-thinking-coach` skill (shipped with this repo).
2. Each chapter ends with an **AI exercise**: copy a prompt from `11_AI_Prompt_Pack`, or just tell the AI "critique this meeting note using the consulting-thinking matrix".
3. Use **one of your own real meetings** as the material, and level up.

## Chapter 1 From recording to judgment
**Key point**: recording is a baseline skill (C1), not performance. Meeting note → problem (C2) → cause (C3) → solution (C4) → consulting judgment (C5). Most people are stuck at C1.
**North star**: "I don't need you to remember what the client said; I need you to judge what the client actually needs, and how we win the next step."
Source: `01 §3`.
> **AI exercise**: hand the AI your most recent meeting note: "**Score this with the rubric — what C-level am I, and why**" (prompt P5). Record your starting point.

## Chapter 2 The matrix
**Key point**: subjects use 3C (including **us, TigerAI** — this corrects the blind spot of only analyzing the client); Environment (Place) is a structural lens laid over every row. The nine cells are only a map, not the destination. **If you only fill the nine cells, you are still organizing information; only when you reason across cells do you begin to enter consulting judgment.**
Source: `01 §1`.
> **AI exercise**: "**Lay this meeting into the 3-subject × 3-tense matrix — which cells are empty?**" (prompt P3)

## Chapter 3 Three readings and the diagonal ★
**Key point**: vertical = vector (derivation, not snapshot); horizontal = gap (opportunity); **the diagonal = the consulting fee**. Recommendations almost always run along a diagonal: the competitor's "present" blind spot × the client's "future" need × the environment's "past" shift.
Source: `01 §2`.
> **AI exercise**: "**Which diagonal can this meeting connect into? Write it as one recommendation**" (prompt P3). **This is the single most important exercise in the book.**

## Chapter 4 The unspoken need
**Key point**: the client describes a past pain in present language; the consultant hears the future need they cannot articulate. Kano: today's delighter → tomorrow's expectation → the day-after's basic — needs evolve over time. This cell cannot be back-filled or outsourced.
Source: `01 §1 ★ cell` + `06` (Kano integration).
> **AI exercise**: "**What might the client not have said this time but could need in future? Mark which parts are signal and which are inference**".

## Chapter 5 The consulting-style meeting summary
**Key point**: six-block format (verbatim / real pain / present need / future + unspoken / competitive environment / how we enter) + next step + one diagonal.
Source / template: `02`.
> **AI exercise**: "**Critique this meeting note with the consulting-thinking matrix**" (prompt P1). Compare the AI's output with your own.

## Chapter 6 Self-scoring and the capping rule
**Key point**: six items, 0–2 each; **no diagonal → capped at C3**; only answering what the client asked → C4 at most. Guards against the "illusion of completeness".
Source: `03`.
> **AI exercise**: "**Score me with the rubric and tell me which cell to fill to move up one level**" (prompt P2).

## Chapter 7 Good vs bad
**Key point**: the same case at C1 (2/12) vs C5 (12/12); the difference is entirely in block 4 and that one diagonal.
Source: `08`.
> **AI exercise**: compare your Chapter-5 summary with the good version in `08`, and ask the AI "**what am I still missing to reach C5**".

## Chapter 8 Manager coaching
**Key point**: training order = first draw a diagonal across the client row → add competitor → add environment / us; use Socratic questions, not scolding; one external-input task per week to cure the knowledge gap.
Source: `04`.
> **AI exercise (for managers)**: "**A report came in leaning C1 — generate 4 questions to push them toward future / competitor / environment / us**" (prompt P4).

## Chapter 9 Client deliverables
**Key point**: same thinking, but externally reveal only the part valuable to the client (post-meeting summary / insight memo / pre-proposal diagnosis / account plan); internal calculations stay internal.
Source: `12`.
> **AI exercise**: "**Turn my internal summary into a client-facing insight memo, hiding internal commercial calculations**".

## Chapter 10 Turn it into your AI coach + a team mechanism
**Key point**: the daily loop = weekly meeting → submit a `02` summary → AI / manager scores with `03` + challenges with `04` → watch "the diagonal / block 4" to track C1→C5. Scale across the team with the `consulting-thinking-coach` skill.
> **Graduation exercise**: go back to the starting point you recorded in Chapter 1 and score again. **How many diagonals does your output now produce — from 0 to how many?**
